UX & UI Design Google Home Page

Recreating Chrome's Home Page Using Figma.

As part of my journey to become a better UX and UI Designer, learning a new tool becomes a necessary learning curve that one has to endure. Already being proficient in using Adobe's XD and Zeppelin, having some commercial experience using XD both tool's, another tool that other designer's were persuading me to learn was called Figma. Figma on one hand, is similar to the aforementioned tools in the sense that - yes, you can design Wireframes, High Fidelity designs, design Vectors make and share prototypes alike. However, Figma offers a feature that unlocks the boundaries that all of the other tools have and that is responsive design using Auto-Layer! Auto Layer, if you have some development experience is basically Flex for your designs. Adding and removing constraints through Fluid containers for any screen size, all in all making designing more efficient and precise.

Please read on through this Case Study on recreating Google Chromes homepage using Figma - Thank You and please enjoy!

Case Summary

Product Vision

The vision for this case study was to establish a clear understanding on using Figma as a design tool to expand my knowledge in UI and UX Design. Focusing on:

  • Develop my skills in design using Figma's software to enhance Fluid and Responsive design for multiple viewports.
  • Aim to consolidate everything into one single design which will cater for all viewports.
  • To answer this, I had to research development that Figma has made to cater for responsive design.
  • Lastly, collect the data from this research and recreate a responsive design of Google Chrome's Home Page around this data.

Discovered User Problem

The issue that designers have whilst using some Design software, is although their end designs are very well engineered and User tested, the software itself - in regards to Fluid Design was found to be either non existent or sub standard.
I intended to research a previously unexplored Design Software called Figma, having being influenced by other designers and intrigued from research conducted.
The aim was to eliminate 'break point designs' and have one single responsive and interactive design that developers could efficiently utilise on handover. It was discovered that although Figma did provide a tool called, 'Auto-Layer,' which encapsulated containers into a more flexible container for a more fluid control over responsiveness, it didn't cater completely and therefore a single page design was not possible at the time of writing (2021).

Outcomes

Tasked with researching and developing new skills in using a new Design Software, naming Figma, to create a single paged interactive Fluid Design rather than having the usual - three non-responsive break point design. Research discovered that Figma does have a feature that can cater for such a task as an integral part of its Software, called, "Auto Layer." As the name suggests it automatically adds a layer to the selected items, which when used correctly, encapsulates them into a fluid child-container. Which, by itself add's constraints and enforced it to behave responsively either in a column or row on user interaction. This does add a further dimension to designing, when this is also added to the parent container, it plays its part and makes the whole design responsive.

It does have a major issue though. At the time of writing (2021), it was discovered that it's not completely the answer. As you will you see from this case study, the "Auto Layer," feature will only work up to a certain point, whereby the content inside is no longer responding effectively and a new breakpoint is required. Concluding that although having just one single interactive and responsive design was not possible yet. That being said it will make for more efficient designing and development having only needing two breakpoint designs which are also responsive and interactive.

Discovery and Ideation

I began by researching the existing market on Design Software that included fluid design when the user interacted. After some investigation and analysis of 5 of the most popular Design Software available on the market to date (2021), surprisingly only one product catered for fluid interactive design. What was more surprising was that this type of feature more than likely in the a marketing sense, would be a "paid for," feature, certainly Adobe although they do have a time limited version of their Design Software available on the market, wouldn't shy away from charging for this type of feature for their product. Figma's free version comes with this for all available versions.

What followed were efforts to discover which software offered more design tools, which had the best user reviews and more importantly which software offered support to eliminate the break point design system that would enable to have one responsive and interactive design as the end goal, or as a maximum requirement, would have the less if break points as possible:

Desk Research

Discovery Analysis

Carrying out Desk Research on the contenders with relatable Software on the market today for Design, moreover, which offered the best experience over all and which had the aforementioned Fluid Design feature. Below are the discovered 4 main contenders following the Desk Research and which products had the greater Statistical Deviation following the customer reviews in terms of User Experience:

  • Adobe XD
  • Figma
  • inVision
  • Affinity
Following the Customer Review research for each of the four contenders the following was scrutinised from this various forums and online product reviews:

Adobe XD

Adobe XD whilst certainly the most successful for Longevity by consistently adapting and updating, an equal amount of good and bad reviews were discovered through out.
The Pros: Simplistic UI, Low Learning Curve, Sharing And Cross Platform Capabilities, 'Repeat Grid' Makes Designing In This Respect Most Efficient.
The Cons: No Security To Protect The Mock Ups When Sharing, No Tools For Animating Elements (Key Frames), Poor Video Quality When Prototyping. ❌ No Fluid Design Feature.

Figma

Figma is a vector graphics editor and prototyping tool which is primarily web-based, with additional offline features enabled by desktop applications for macOS and Windows.
The Pros: Low Learning Curve, Simplistic UI, Cross Platform aAnd Collaboration, Auto Layout Prototyping.
The Cons: Both Web And Software Are Memory Load Heavy, No Built In Animation Tools (Keyframes), Converted CSS Code Is Below Standard. ✔ Fluid Design Feature Found!

inVision

InVision is a prototyping, collaboration and workflow tool.
The Pros: Prototyping, Supports Gestures, Web Ready Transitions And Animations Available, Cross Platform And Collaboration, Plug-ins, Online Community, Full Version Control
The Cons:Confusing And Complex UI, Sharing Settings And Toolbar Are Not Always Intuitive, Poor HCI, Element Inspector Is Not Always Precise. ❌ No Fluid Design Feature.

Affinity

Affinity Designer is a vector graphics editor by Serif.
The Pros: Intuitive UI, Modern Gestures, Great Artistic Tools, Cross Platform
The Cons:Needs Paid Subscription To Export, Poor Customer Support, Complicated And Clunky, Steeper learning curve compared to other software. ❌ No Fluid Design Feature.

Idea Prioritisation

Based on discovery research findings I came up with three hypothesis to ensure successful product adoption. These covered:

  • The majority of software have no fluid interaction so a planned thesis is to keep the UI simple, with one container in the center with responsive elements contained inside for screen adaptation.
  • Visual hierarchy for the product.
  • Replicate a well known home page with a simple UI and little to no background to allow for visualisation of the overall aim.
  • A visually pleasing and interactive review experience.
  • A user friendly user experience given the learning limitation that the software has for new user's.

Prototyping

After some research and contemplation, it was decided that Google Chromes Homepage was the most simplistic and most familiar to users. This was because the majority of Internet users trended towards the browser and has done for the majority of time too. Overall that trend, at time of writing (2021), stood at 65.27% of the global share of the market! That is for example, more than 6 in 10 people use this browser over any other - a sure sign that its overall simplicity, familiarity, accessability and usability surpass every other browser available today.

Rapid Prototyping

During Rapid Prototyping, several iterations generated during a short period of time, the features were created and tested in an attempt to optimize characteristics like shape, size, and overall usability based on user feedback and analysis. Final tests produced the final prototype and the Natural User Flow had been established.
